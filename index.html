    Chapter 3: Basic Flight Maneuvers — Sparknotes

    The foundation — straight-and-level, turns, climbs, descents. Everything else is a variation.

    The gist — 5 bullets
    • Attitude + Power = Performance. Set the picture, set the power, trim, verify.
    • Outside first, inside second. Use instruments to confirm, not to fly.
    • Every aileron input needs a rudder input.
    • Trim, trim, trim. If you're pulling or pushing, you're not trimmed.
    • Small corrections. Fingertips, not fists.

    The Four Fundamentals

    Every maneuver you'll ever fly is a combination of these four. Master them and landings, stalls, ground reference — it's all just variation.

    Straight-and-levelConstant heading AND constant altitude
    TurnsHorizontal component of lift turns the airplane
    ClimbsPitch up + add power
    DescentsReduce power or pitch down to lose altitude

    Core Concepts

    Flight controls

    • Ailerons — roll
    • Elevator — pitch
    • Rudder — yaw
    • Throttle — power; with pitch, determines performance
    • Trim — relieves control pressure; doesn't replace the input
    Attitude + Power = Performance

    Set a known pitch and bank at a known power setting → known performance. This is the whole game.

    Feel of the airplane

    Recognize airplane state through sight, sound (airflow, engine note), and kinesthetic feel (control pressures, seat-of-the-pants). Don't fixate on instruments — develop outside awareness first.

    Integrated flight instruction

    From day one, use both outside visual references AND instrument cross-check. Builds habits for instrument training later.

    Straight-and-Level Flight

    Constant heading AND constant altitude. Reference point: the natural horizon against a consistent spot on the cowling or glareshield.

    • Level pitch — consistent cowling-to-horizon reference
    • Wings level — wingtips equidistant from the horizon
    • Coordination — ball centered; rudder offsets torque and P-factor
    • Trim — always trim after establishing attitude, never before

    Scan: ~90% outside, ~10% instruments. The altimeter, heading, and VSI confirm what your eyes already told you.

    Level Turns

    Turns are banked — the horizontal component of lift turns the airplane.

    Shallow (<20°)Airplane tends to roll wings level on its own
    Medium (20–45°)Neutral — bank stays where you put it
    Steep (>45°)Overbanking — hold opposite aileron to stop the roll

    Key issues

    • Adverse yaw — up-going aileron creates extra drag, yawing the nose opposite the turn. Correct with rudder into the turn on roll-in.
    • Load factor — lift must increase to hold altitude in a bank. Add back-pressure and a touch of power in steeper banks. Stall speed rises with bank angle.
    • Overbanking — at >45°, the outside wing travels faster and lifts more. Hold opposite aileron.
    • Rollout — lead by about half the bank angle (30° bank → start rollout ~15° before target heading).
    • Coordination — ball centered. Slipping (too little rudder) and skidding (too much rudder) are both dangerous, especially at low altitude.

    Climbs and Climbing Turns

    Increasing altitude. Pitch up AND add power.

    VyBest rate of climb — most altitude per unit time. Normal climb.
    VxBest angle of climb — most altitude per unit distance. Obstacle clearance.
    Cruise climbLower pitch, higher airspeed, better engine cooling and visibility.

    Procedure: raise the nose to the climb attitude → apply climb power → trim → verify airspeed.

    Watch for

    Torque and P-factor are strongest at low airspeed + high power → expect right-rudder need. In climbing turns, use shallow bank (≤20°) — steep banks rob climb performance.

    Descents and Descending Turns

    Three flavors, each with a purpose:

    • Partial-power (cruise) descent — reduce power, maintain cruise airspeed, ~500 fpm typical. Standard enroute descent.
    • Glide (power-off) — idle power, pitch for best-glide speed. Used to simulate engine failure and practice emergency approach.
    • Minimum safe airspeed descent — slow flight descent, used near obstacles.

    Key: in a prolonged glide, ease the throttle up briefly every so often to clear the engine — prevents plug fouling and shock cooling. Expect left-yawing tendency to diminish at low power.

    Pitch and Power

    Common teaching: pitch controls airspeed, power controls altitude — especially on approach. In reality they're coupled; you rarely change one without the other. In steady-state cruise, trim for a pitch that gives you the airspeed you want at the power setting you chose.

    Common Errors

    Watch yourself for
    • Fixating on one instrument instead of scanning
    • Not trimming — fighting the airplane
    • Uncoordinated turns (ball off-center)
    • Forgetting rudder on roll-in / roll-out
    • Letting pitch wander during bank changes
    • Using ailerons instead of rudder for heading corrections
    • Chasing altitude with big, jerky inputs
    • Not leading rollouts and level-offs

    Bottom-Line Takeaways

    1. Attitude + Power = Performance. Set the picture, set the power, trim, verify.
    2. Outside first, inside second. Use instruments to confirm, not to fly.
    3. Coordinate with rudder. Every aileron input needs a rudder input.
    4. Trim, trim, trim. If you're pulling or pushing, you're not trimmed.
    5. Small corrections. Fly with fingertips, not fists.

    FAR Reference · ATC · KSNA

    FAR Reference, ATC Communications & KSNA Operations — Sparknotes

    Every Part 61 / Part 91 reg Desireé has flagged across GL1–GL3, plus the radio + taxi fluency you need for John Wayne.

    The gist — 6 bullets
    • 61.113 core: No PPL acts as PIC for compensation or hire. Memorize the 7 exceptions (b–h).
    • Pro-rata only. Pilot pays AT LEAST equal share. Fuel/oil/airport fees/rental only.
    • ATIS first, every flight. Info letter, time, wind, vis, ceiling, temp/dew, altimeter, runways, NOTAMs.
    • "DIVE AWAY" for crosswind taxi. Push yoke forward + turn AWAY from the wind direction.
    • KSNA mandatory 15° offset to heading 181° on RWY 20L departure (wake turbulence).
    • Niner, not nine. Roger ≠ yes. Wilco = will comply. Plain English OK for non-standard requests.

    FAR 61.113 — Private Pilot Privileges & Limitations

    The single most important reg on the checkride oral. Be able to recite the core rule and at least three exceptions cold.

    No PPL may act as PIC of an aircraft carrying passengers or property for compensation or hire.

    The 7 Exceptions (b–h)

    (b) Incidental to businessFlying must be INCIDENTAL — NOT the primary business function. Doctor flying for supplies = OK. Charter pilot = NOT OK.
    (c) Pro-rata cost sharingPilot pays AT LEAST an equal share. 3 ppl, $300 → $100 each. Only fuel/oil/airport fees/rental.
    (d) Charitable event flightPer FAR 91.146. 100% reimbursement of fuel/oil/airport fees/rental allowed.
    (e) Search & rescueReimbursement allowed ONLY when sanctioned by an agency AND agency initiates contact.
    (f) Aircraft salesman demoNeed ≥200 hours logged PIC time before demoing aircraft to a prospective buyer.
    (g) Glider/ultralight towAllowed with FAR 61.69 endorsement (CFI training + checkride for towing operation).
    (h) Light-sport production testPowered parachute / weight-shift control: ≥100 hours PIC in category, familiar with test ops.
    Gray area to flag

    SkyAccess operator-outreach flights — does meeting with operators count as compensation under (b)? Desireé flagged this as needing written guidance from Sergio / chief instructor. Document the answer for your records.

    Cite-the-reg drill

    Examiners love "what reg covers ___?" Practice both directions: number → rule, AND rule → number. Use the FAR/AIM digital app ($20, updates annually) — never the printed book, which Desireé says is outdated the day it ships.

    ATIS — Towered Field Weather

    Information letter A→Z (rolls hourly). Always state your info letter on initial contact with each new controller.

    • Read order: Info letter · Zulu time · wind dir/speed (G for gust) · visibility (SM) · ceiling/coverage (FEW/SCT/BKN/OVC) · temp · dewpoint · altimeter · runways in use · remarks/NOTAMs.
    • Non-towered fields: AWOS or ASOS instead. Identifier shown in ForeFlight.
    • Three ways to consume: (1) call the ATIS phone number, (2) ForeFlight text, (3) ForeFlight audio. Practice with the LIVE phone call to build transcription speed.
    • Practice drill: Call SNA ATIS while driving to the airport and transcribe in shorthand.

    KSNA Frequencies — memorize this table

    ATIS126.0 — listen FIRST, get the letter, runway in use, altimeter, and which clearance freq is active.
    Clearance Delivery118.0 (primary) · 121.85 (secondary) — use whichever ATIS tells you to.
    Ground120.8 (primary) · 132.25 (secondary) — switch here after clearance for taxi.
    Tower119.9 (primary, RWY 2-20) · 126.8 (split ops) · 128.35 (secondary).
    SoCal Departure124.1 — Tower hands you off after takeoff for VFR flight following.
    SoCal Approach120.55 — inbound; pick up before entering the Class C shelf.
    ATIS → Clearance → Ground → Tower → Departure

    Standard outbound flow. Reverse on the way back: Approach → Tower → Ground → ramp.

    KSNA Specifics

    RunwaysParallel 20L / 20R. Prevailing winds 210–260° drive the choice.
    Mandatory offset15° LEFT turn to heading 181° at end of RWY 20L departure (wake turbulence).
    Pattern altitude856 ft AGL published; fly 900.
    Turn-to-heading altitude500 ft AGL practical (technically 556 → 600 ft per FAR 91.181).
    Run-upSE run-up CLOSED. Compass Road CLOSED. Always request MIDFIELD via Alpha-Hotel U-turn.
    CautionOpposite-direction taxi traffic on shared taxiways. Slow to 3–5 kt, move RIGHT, pass, resume.
    Practice areaEl Toro.

    Clearance Delivery Script (KSNA 118.0)

    "Clearance, Skyhawk 51840 is a Cessna 172 Uniform at Dove Street requesting VFR flight following to [destination] with information [letter]."

    Write it on the ForeFlight scratch pad and read verbatim until automatic. KSNA controllers don't have time for hesitation.

    Equipment Suffix

    Aircraft type + slash + equipment code (e.g., 172/U). Uniform = basic equipment, no ILS. Tells ATC what approaches/procedures you can accept.

    Readback Discipline

    • Use shorthand, NOT full sentences. Example notes: Left 080 / 2400 / 124.1 / Squawk 0213
    • Readback: "left 080, at or below 2400, 124.1, squawk 0213, Skyhawk 51840."
    • Heading / altitude / frequency / squawk MUST be exact.
    • Full call sign on first contact with each controller; you may match abbreviated form once they shorten ("840").

    Phraseology

    "Niner"Always for 9 (not "nine") — distinguishes from "five" on a noisy frequency.
    "Five" / "Three"Just "five" / "three" — NOT "fife" / "tree."
    Roger"I copy / acknowledged" — NOT "yes."
    Affirmative / NegativeYes / No.
    Wilco"Will comply + acknowledged." Useful to shorten long readbacks.
    Plain EnglishOK for non-standard requests (fuel, restaurant directions). Reduces frequency congestion.

    Tower Handoff

    Monitor [freq]Switch frequency but DO NOT transmit. Just listen.
    Contact [freq]Switch frequency AND transmit a position report.
    "Say altitude"Wants your CURRENT altitude (not assigned). "Skyhawk 51840, 1400 climbing to 2400."

    Phonetic Alphabet

    Alpha · Bravo · Charlie · Delta · Echo · Foxtrot · Golf · Hotel · India · Juliet · Kilo · Lima · Mike · November · Oscar · Papa · Quebec · Romeo · Sierra · Tango · Uniform · Victor · Whiskey · X-ray · Yankee · Zulu

    Taxi Operations

    • Jurisdictions: Clearance = pre-flight at parking. Ground = parking → runway holding point. Tower = runway and pattern.
    • Ground readiness call: "Ground, Skyhawk 51840 at Dove Street request midfield run-up."
    • Pedals: Heels = rudder, toes = brakes. Differential braking + throttle for tight turns.
    • Crosswind taxi (DIVE AWAY): Push yoke FORWARD and turn AWAY from the wind direction. Wind from left → push forward + turn right.
    • Windsock: Fully inflated ≈ 15 kt. Each stripe ≈ 3 kt. Opening points DOWNWIND; inflation points INTO the wind.

    Six-Pack & Systems Refresher

    • Six-pack: Attitude · Altimeter · VSI · Airspeed · Heading Indicator · Turn Coordinator (turn rate + ball).
    • "Step on the ball": Ball right of center → step right rudder. Ball left → step left rudder.
    • C172 flap schedule: Flaps 10° below 110 kt; Flaps 20° and 30° below 85 kt (full = landing config).
    • Runway naming: Magnetic bearing ÷ 10 + L/R/C suffix. 20L = ~200° magnetic, parallel left.

    Bottom-Line Takeaways

    1. Memorize 61.113. Core rule + all 7 exceptions (b–h). DPE will probe.
    2. Write the clearance script. Read it verbatim on the radio until it's automatic.
    3. "Niner" for 9. Roger ≠ yes. Wilco saves time.
    4. DIVE AWAY for crosswind taxi. Ailerons INTO the wind, yoke forward.
    5. KSNA quirks: 15° offset to 181°, midfield run-up, opposite-direction taxi.
    6. Get the FAA written DONE early — Sergio's preference, before serious flight training.

    METARs, Runway Numbering, Hazardous Attitudes & Runway Incursions — Sparknotes

    Reading weather out loud, the math behind runway numbers, the five attitudes that kill pilots, and why we read back every clearance.

    The gist — 6 bullets
    • METARs are read OUT LOUD. Direction digit-by-digit. Wind is "AT". Vis >6 = P6SM. AO2 = with precip discrim. RMK = remarks (AIM 5-1-3).
    • Runway number = magnetic bearing ÷ 10. Reciprocal = ±20. L/C/R from the approach side. Always say both digits.
    • Five hazardous attitudes — memorize all five + every antidote. The written test pairs them.
    • Runway incursion = wrong presence on a protected surface. 4 severity levels (A worst → D best). Read back EVERY clearance — that is the Tenerife lesson.
    • Movement = ATC controls. Non-movement = you don't ask. Solid yellow line = non-movement side; dashed = movement.
    • Sterile cockpit during taxi. No phone, no chatter. ATC can amend at any moment.

    METAR Decoding — Read It Like ATC Reads It

    METAR = the routine hourly weather observation. Standard issue at the :55 mark. SPECI ("special") gets issued mid-hour when conditions change drastically. ATIS pulls from the same data, which is why the ATIS letter advances mid-hour after a SPECI.

    "METAR for [airport] on the [DD]th day at [HHMM] Zulu, winds [DDD] at [SS], gusting [GG] knots, [V] statute miles of visibility, [coverage] at [altitude], temperature [T], dewpoint [D], altimeter [A.AA], remarks [...]."

    The Field-by-Field Read

    Date / TimeFirst six digits = DDHHMM Zulu (e.g., 240053Z = 24th day, 00:53Z). Always say "on the 24th day at 00:53 Zulu."
    WindDDDSSGGGKT — direction digit-by-digit ("one eight zero" not "one-eighty"). Wind is "AT". Gust is the upper number — never "at 11 to 18."
    VisibilityBelow 10: spoken plain ("3 statute miles of visibility"). Past 10: encoded as P6SM (Plus 6 Statute Miles) — never "17 statute miles."
    Cloud coverageFEW 1–2 oktas · SCT scattered 3–4 · BKN broken 5–7 · OVC overcast 8/8. Spoken: "few clouds at 2,700, scattered at 9,000."
    Temp / dewpointTwo-digit Celsius each. "Temperature 19, dewpoint 13." The T-group inside RMK gives precision to the tenth (T01890128 = 18.9 / 12.8).
    AltimeterA2986 = 29.86" Hg. Spoken plain numeric: "altimeter 2986" (no decimal point in voice).
    RemarksRMK = remarks block. Per AIM 5-1-3 contractions table. Includes T-group, SLP (sea-level pressure), wind-shift codes.

    AO1 vs AO2 — know cold

    AO1 = Automated Station WITHOUT precipitation discrimination · AO2 = Automated Station WITH precipitation discrimination

    AO2 stations can tell rain from snow. Almost every modern automated station is AO2.

    Weather Descriptor Codes (memorize the common merges)

    TSThunderstorm
    FZFreezing
    BLBlowing
    DRDrift (low blowing)
    MIShallow
    BRMist ("baby rain" — no good reason for the code, just memorize)
    PRPartial
    SHShower
    SQSquall
    FCFunnel cloud
    DZ / RA / SNDrizzle / Rain / Snow
    GR / GSHail / Small hail
    FG / HZ / FUFog / Haze / Smoke

    Combinations: BLDU = blowing widespread dust · -RA = light rain · +TSRA = heavy thunderstorm with rain.

    Practice drill

    DPEs pull from places that actually have weather — Wyoming, Denver, the Midwest. Pull a different airport in ForeFlight every day and read the METAR out loud at speed. Do this on the drive in like you do with ATIS.

    Runway Numbering & Reciprocals

    Runway number = magnetic bearing of the centerline, rounded to the nearest 10°, with the last digit dropped. Heading 198° rounds to 200° → "Runway 20." Heading 022° → "Runway 02."

    RWY # = round(magnetic bearing) ÷ 10 · Reciprocal = ±20 (drop the leading zero)

    The Rules

    • Always say BOTH digits. "Two zero" not "twenty." ATC will never clear you to "Runway 200" or "Runway 90" — it's two-zero or zero-niner-zero.
    • Lowest possible: 01 (heading 010°). Highest: 36 (heading 360°). Never single digit.
    • Reciprocal math: add 20 to the "right" half (or subtract from the "left" half) — that's the drop-the-zero version of ±180°. 020 ↔ 200 · 060 ↔ 240 · 090 ↔ 270.
    • Parallel suffix L/C/R assigned from the approach side. KSNA has 20L / 20R; when winds shift, the same strips become 02L / 02R.
    • KSNA winds favor 20L/20R 99% of the time. Coastal headwind on departure, tailwind on arrival is rare.

    When to Spell vs Speak Plain

    Headings · Runway #s · SquawksDigit-by-digit. "Heading zero eight zero." "Squawk zero two one three."
    Speeds · Altitudes · Visibility (≤10)Spoken plain. "100 knots." "VFR at or below 2500." "10 statute miles of visibility."
    Relative degree offsetsSpoken plain. "15-degree offset" (because it's relative to your current heading, not an absolute heading).

    CFIT — Controlled Flight Into Terrain

    You fly a perfectly airworthy airplane into the ground. No engine failure, no mechanical problem — just a decision-making failure. The textbook setup: VFR-only pilot pushes into deteriorating weather, gets into IMC, becomes spatially disoriented, descends into a mountain.

    KSNA local hazard

    Santiago Peak (5,689 ft) collects orographic clouds. A cross-country east on a marginal day is the classic CFIT setup. If you're not instrument rated and the weather is closing, turn around before you're committed.

    If You Get Inadvertently IMC

    "Mayday, mayday, mayday — Skyhawk 51840 — IMC, request vectors out of cloud, no longer VFR."

    Declare immediately. Don't try to fly out yourself. ATC will vector you. CFIT lives in the enVironment + External pressures slots of PAVE — sometimes Pilot (fatigue, get-there-itis).

    The Five Hazardous Attitudes (memorize verbatim)

    The FAA identifies five hazardous attitudes that degrade pilot decision-making. Each has a one-line dangerous thought and a one-line antidote. The written test pairs them — DPE shows the dangerous thought, you pick the antidote (or vice versa).

    1. Anti-authority"Don't tell me." → Antidote: "Follow the rules. They are usually right."
    2. Impulsivity"Do it now." → Antidote: "Not so fast. Think first."
    3. Invulnerability"It won't happen to me." → Antidote: "It could happen to me."
    4. Macho"I can do it." → Antidote: "Taking chances is foolish."
    5. Resignation"What's the use?" → Antidote: "I'm not helpless. I can make a difference."
    Self-assessment matters

    Know your own default. Aryan's read on himself: anti-authority + macho, with resignation lowest. Desireé's: impulsivity — and it cost her a CFI checkride. She extended upwind, debated in her head whether she heard the call right, didn't use the G1000 audio playback button, turned downwind early, conflicted with sequenced traffic. Examiner: "girlfriend, it was perfect — I have to bust you for this." When in doubt about a clearance, USE the playback or ASK. Never act on what you THINK you heard.

    Runway Incursions

    "Any occurrence at an aerodrome involving the incorrect presence of an aircraft, vehicle, or person on the protected area of a surface designated for the landing and takeoff of aircraft."

    Memorize verbatim. Aerodrome = "a defined area on land or water, including buildings, installations, and equipment, intended for the arrival, departure, and movement of aircraft." Both definitions are FAA verbatim.

    Four Severity Categories

    ABarely-avoided collision
    BSignificant potential for collision
    CAmple time and distance to avoid collision
    DLittle to no risk of collision

    KSNA Incursion Risks

    • Morning back-taxi. Before noise abatement opens (~7 AM), airliners use the runway as a taxiway in sequence. Cessnas hold and wait.
    • Opposite-direction taxi. KSNA Cessna ops share a single taxiway with oncoming aircraft. Ground announces "caution opposite-direction Cessna." Both move RIGHT, pass nose-to-nose, resume.
    • Southwest off Hotel. Airliners normally exit at Echo or Delta. When they unexpectedly exit at Hotel, you can get squeezed.
    • Echo crossings. Going Alpha-Hotel to midfield run-up — watch for "hold short of Echo for Southwest. Caution jet blast."

    Hot Spots

    FAA-charted areas of elevated incursion risk. Marked on the airport diagram and in the chart supplement. KSNA has HS1 and HS2 (between the runways). Hot spots = "extra eyes here," not "avoid." Note: the midfield run-up area in front of Hotel was a hot spot until recently. They removed the designation when construction started, but the risk hasn't changed — treat it like a hot spot anyway.

    Tenerife — the textbook why-we-read-back-clearances story

    1977, Los Rodeos (now Tenerife North), KLM 4805 + Pan Am 1736. 583 fatalities — the worst aviation disaster in history. Low-vis day, runway being used as a taxiway. KLM started take-off roll without clearance while Pan Am was still back-taxiing on the same runway. KLM tried to rotate early, near-clear with a tail strike, crashed on top of Pan Am. Always read back every clearance verbatim. If unsure, ask.

    Movement vs Non-Movement Areas

    Movement area"All taxiways and runways under the jurisdiction of the control tower." Clearance required to enter. ATC controls.
    Non-movement areaRamps and parking areas. ATC does NOT control. No clearance or radio call needed.
    SOLID yellow line = boundary; non-movement side · DASHED yellow line = movement side

    Mnemonic: "dash = movement, solid = stopped."

    Practical Cases

    • Pulling the plane out of the tie-down at Dove Street to a parking spot = non-movement, no call.
    • Taxiing onto Charlie or Hotel = movement, ground clearance required.
    • Wing/wheel will overhang the boundary while ground-handling on foot? Grab a handheld, tune ground, give a courtesy call: "Ground, just FYI we're shifting an aircraft at Dove Street, the wing will overhang the movement boundary." Sometimes they say "stand by" if traffic is congested.

    Taxi Workload — Sterile Cockpit

    • No phone. No app fiddling. No non-essential talk. ATC can amend your clearance any moment ("hold short Echo for Southwest, caution jet blast"). Miss the call = incursion.
    • Take-runway flow — "lights, camera, action": all exterior lights ON, mixture FULL RICH, transponder/squawk confirmed.
    • At any intersection: "clear left, clear straight, clear right" OUT LOUD, head moving. Same logic as a car at an uncontrolled intersection.
    • If unsure of a clearance: use the G1000 audio playback or query ATC ("Confirm hold short Echo, Skyhawk 51840"). They'd rather repeat than have an incursion.

    ATC Handoffs, Flight Following & Guard

    • "Babysitter" model. Whoever you're talking to NOW is your babysitter. Need to amend? Talk to your CURRENT controller — never reach back to a previous one.
    • KSNA outbound flow: Clearance → Ground → Tower → SoCal Departure (124.1).
    • KSNA inbound flow: SoCal → Tower → Ground.
    • Flight following = TRACON VFR advisory service. Optional in most airspace. KSNA requires you to depart with flight following.
    • Quiet periods are normal. Once you're in the El Toro practice area, SoCal often won't talk to you for long stretches. They're still there — they'll call traffic if it matters.
    • Guard frequency 121.5 = universal emergency. Every aircraft monitors it. If you go down or call mayday on guard, every plane within range hears you. Monitor it when not on flight following.

    AIM Reading Map

    AIM Chapter 5Air Traffic Procedures — clearances, handoffs, separation. Section 5-1-3 = contractions table (RMK, AO2, SLP, etc.).
    AIM Chapter 7Safety of Flight — weather services, hazardous weather, in-flight weather advisories.
    AIM Chapter 14Airport Operations — signs, runway/taxiway markings, lighting. Required reading before next ground. DPE will pull a sign image and ask "taxiway or runway?"

    Bottom-Line Takeaways

    1. Read METARs out loud daily. Different airport every time. Direction digit-by-digit, "AT" for wind, P6SM for vis >6.
    2. Runway # = magnetic ÷ 10. Reciprocal = ±20. Always say both digits.
    3. Memorize the five hazardous attitudes + every antidote. The written test pairs them.
    4. Read back every clearance. Unsure? Use playback or ask. Don't be Tenerife.
    5. Dash = movement, solid = stopped.
    6. Sterile cockpit during taxi. Lights camera action. Clear left, straight, right.
    7. Read AIM Ch 14 before next lesson. Sign and marking memorization.
